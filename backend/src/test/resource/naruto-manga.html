Synopsis

Before Naruto's birth, a great demon fox had attacked the Hidden Leaf Village. A man known as the 4th Hokage sealed the demon inside the newly born Naruto, causing him to unknowingly grow up detested by his fellow villagers. Despite his lack of talent in many areas of ninjutsu, Naruto strives for only one goal: to gain the title of Hokage, the strongest ninja in his village. Desiring the respect he never received, Naruto works toward his dream with fellow friends Sasuke and Sakura and mentor Kakashi as they go through many trials and battles that come with being a ninja.

Coming up with precise numbers to essentially give a value to a series that spans 700 chapters and well over a decade in production is not an easy thing. Much has changed over the years of publication, but I guess overall I would give the series a "very good".

Like many other people, the Naruto anime was my introduction to the world of anime--something that literally changed the course of my life. The storyline of a young boy who had the courage (or more like the gall) to always stay confident and continue smiling despite growing up alone in a community that openly detested him is heartwarming to say the least. I liked that the series also introduced a wide range of characters--Kishimoto really succeeded in creating not just a village, but an entire world within his story.

STORYLINE:
As one would expect from a 700 chapter-long series, the series has its highs and its lows. For me, the peak was pretty early in the series throughout the Chunin exam arc, so it was a little bit of a disappointment that the series never really made it back to that high later on. Then again, the main trio of Naruto, Sakura and Sasuke weren't nearly my favourite characters, so maybe my problem was that I didn't care about the people who mattered the most. Having said that, the fact that I was still interested enough to keep reading should speak for the story's quality. The chapters are relatively short (compared to monthly publications) and almost all of them end with some sort of cliffhanger, so I ended up stopping at points to save up for longer reading sessions.

CHARACTERS:
On the topic of characters, I would have to argue that they are far from perfect. He did a good job of creating diversity in his characters and strong, complex personalities in many, but there were many characters who were sadly quite shafted and remained static throughout the series (I'm looking at Tenten and Shino). I wouldn't say that ALL his female characters are always weak, submissive and useless, but the series is a far cry from gender-equal. In part I can accept that this is because the manga's intended audience was for young boys who probably don't care if there's gender equality and all that stuff, but still.

ART:
The art... is good. It's definitely improved over time from both experience and technological advancements. I like many of the character designs and I like that they change as the characters mature. Personally I cared more for the social interactions within the story than the fight scenes, so sometimes some chapters of endless fighting were a bit difficult to follow/envision.

ENDING: (contains no specific spoilers, but discussion of overall flow)
Personally I did not like the ending, but I didn't HATE it. When the whole shinobi war started to drag out and literally EVERY CHARACTER WHO WAS EVER SLIGHTLY SIGNIFICANT made an appearance even if they had to defy reality (AND DEATH) to do so, I started looking forward to an end. The ending I got wasn't everything that I wanted, but it was an end. I'm happy that Kishimoto had the guts to see his story to completion including establishing finality with his character relationships (rather than leaving things ambiguous and inconclusive as is the style of most Shonen manga), but I also feel like he crossed the line and forced the pairings too far. I'm also not a fan of the introduction of children (AT ALL.) to imply the relationship of the parents, but at the same time I can't imagine Kishimoto showing us the depth of their relationships in any other way (hand holding, hugging, walking together maybe. Beyond that? No, not even kissing). Regardless, I felt like I had to read the final chapter because "OCD, I have to finish this!" (not to mention my OTP), and did so knowing that I probably wouldn't like much of it. If you don't care for pairings or mentions of children, I'd recommend stopping at 699.

So there you have it, my review that took me 40 mins. I expect most people will have already tl;dr and left this massive review, but if you're still reading it, thank you =) It's a review for myself as much as for anyone else anyways because... it's done. It's over. Naruto's ended. My childhood just ended with it. Huh.

Time to move on with life and keep telling myself that I will NOT be reading the sequel about the children. I won't do it. I won't. (...The lies... I'll cave, I know it. >_>) read more

It's been years since I last read Naruto, I dropped it years ago until 2011 when I heard from a couple of people that the story has picked up in the War arc, so I decided to 're-read the entire manga from start, to the current up-to-date chapter and at last, the final chapter. So far, this very manga broadened my horizons and made me see Naruto in a brand light like no other manga. How come, you may ask? Because I still hate it. Even more after all these excruciating years with that piece of dumpster.

What is Naruto, you may enquire? Naruto is currently one of the leading forces of the shonen demographic along with Bleach and One Piece, and one of the biggest hits by the time it was first published overseas since Dragon Ball. After all, Naruto has everything you would want in a shonen manga. Somewhat decent art, intriguing characters, unique and well thought out fighting styles and last, but not least, ninjas. After all, what's cooler than ninjas who can peform all sorts of wonders?

Unfortunately, those concepts are virtually irrelevant unless they are executed moderately and this is where Naruto fails miserably. I'll assume the reader is already familiar with how these sorts of manga works, but either way I'll explain: Characters, story-propelled messages, and fighting sequences.

Typical shonen cliches comprise the story, peddling messages about not abandoning friends, protecting the motherland at all costs, and the evils of war and hate. This is executed surprisingly well initially, as many characters embody these elements. For example, the main protagonist, Naruto, who starts out as the friendless zero, working his way to become a hero. The Third Hokage being martyred for Konoha and Sasuke, the deuteragonist, whose hatred will consume you. The first arc, Land of Waves, pulls at your heartstrings through excellently timed, but tedious flashbacks that develop the characters and show how they became who they are. This is best during the Chunin Exam arc, where the series reached its peak during the clash between Naruto and Gaara, two foils.

The fights in the first half are well thought out and often brilliant, especially Shikamaru's. The Sasuke Retrieval arc that caps the first half is flawed but explains the motivations of the secondary characters such as Choji, Kiba and Lee, which makes you anticipated for the second half.

Or rather, would have been anticipated if the series didn't start to decline in quality by the time the second half of the series hit.

With the possible exception of the opening of the Kazekage Rescue arc, it's hard to care about the two and a half of years timeskip. The plot pursues the deuteragonist Sasuke, whose fall from grace was swift and straight. Though, while us, the audience, can understand him to some degree and the reasons behind his fall to the darkness, it is not enough for us to sympathize with him. The series devotes whole arcs to Sasuke, making him seem far more important than the titular character, Naruto. But he isn't compelling enough. Naruto, although stupid and bumbling, is a relatable idiot hero. Sasuke's motivations become increasingly counterintuitive, his powers asspulled as “ingenious”.

The fighting sequences started to greatly decline in quality too, to the point where the series essentially became Dragon Ball with Ninjas. Instead of relying on a mixture of Taijutsu and Ninjutsu, we see Naruto spamming his Shadow Clone technique along with a wide variety of Ninjutsu that has the destructive capability to slice mountains as if they were butter and last but not least, acquire the latest wallbanger, Gary Stu powerup. How long did it took Naruto to learn Sage Mode which took Jiraiya decades to learn? 2, 3 days? What is this? That is the one aspect of Naruto that is frustrating me. The author relies on these asspulls to advance the story too, which suffers. Sasuke is the prime example on this matter. The logic with Sasuke is completely retarded. As the series progresses and Naruto keeps getting more powerful than anyone because of the Nine-Tailed Demon Fox, discipline, friendship, hard work and etc, Sasuke needs to stay just as powerful as Naruto or else the manga loses all tension. So Sasuke gains magical power-ups that makes no little to no sense, just so he can stay at the same level as Naruto.

What irritates me even more is that Sasuke is more powerful than Itachi, who was so powerful that he singlehandedly defeated several of his own clansmen. We know that supposedly Itachi and Sasuke are more powerful than average, but why? And why are they so much more powerful? Why is it that all the Uchiha we see are Ubermensch, but all the Uchiha from the past were such pushover fodder? The power creep is so bad that things that were established fact no longer made any sense. How is it that Hashirama and Tobirama even died in the first place, if they are considered gods compared to other men?

All this would haven been justifiable if the characters still developed in coherent directions, but with the exception of a few couple of characters, they don't. Sakura is the shining example. This major character serves no purpose to the plot or anyone. The strong Sakura we saw in the Kazekage Rescue arc was a tease and she has become weak, hesitant, and even more defined by her loathable infatuation with Sasuke. I haven't seen such a horrible, irredeemable character such as her. I'd even go as far as to say Tenten, out of everyone, is more developed than Sakura, who is the apparent female protagonist of the series. That goes as far as to say that Kishimoto pretty much established since the opening of Part II that if you weren't Naruto, Sasuke, a Senju, Uchiha or are in any way related to them, you are rendered irrelevant to the plot.

But that's not the only aspect of Naruto that frustrates me. You want to know what else makes Naruto, the series, ultimately frustrating and insufferable for me? Kishimoto had to do a generational epic HIS way. Generational epics aren't bad, as one of my favorite shonen manga Jojo's Bizarre Adventure is just that, but Kishimoto simply doesn't have Araki's skill or creativity in that area. Whereas Araki gives each generation it's proper development whilst leaving things for the next to improve upon in its own direction, Kishimoto instead builds parallel after parallel.

How many times do we see this or that person referred to as the Naruto, Sasuke and Sakura of that generation? Then there's the feud between Naruto and Sasuke that repeats in Kakashi and Obito, Jiraiya and Orochimaru, Hashirama and Madara, the sons of the Sage of Six Paths. If the ensemble cast was aware of these repetitions, plot mandated they be ineffectual to change little if anything. I started wondering why Kishimoto bothered putting them in the story at all since even if anyone received good amount of development, it more often than not, was done only so it could be tied back to the Senju and Uchiha rift in some way.

That aside, you want to know the other aspect of Naruto which I found appalling? The world building. Unlike Masashi Kishimoto, when J.R.R. Tolkien wrote The Lord of the Rings, he was continuing a job that had been started with the writing of The Hobbit, namely the building of a fictional world. This process involved not only the creation of characters and nations, but also entire cultures and histories, as is exemplified in the many notes Tolkien left behind at the time of his death, the most famous collection of which was published as The Silmarillion.

For the first few hundred chapters of the manga, Kishimoto was content to limit the reader's exposure of the Naruto world to the Land of Fire, the Land of the Waves, and Sunagakure. In the case of the latter village, we had little moments to build up a separate culture from the Leaf through its architecture, its fashions, its rituals, and the motivations of characters from the village. However, as the story went on Kishimoto focused less and less on what made that particular village to the point where Sunagakure was basically Konoha in the desert.

As for the other major villages, despite claims to highly divergent cultures made early on in the manga, we got little of that. There were hints of this with Kirigakure's brutal training regimen, but aside from that, there was little to differentiate the villages. It was made even worse by the fact that, aside from some minor details such the importance of power in Kumogakure, the bloodiness of Yagura, the Fourth Mizukage's regime, and the Will of Stone, thus far little exposure has been given to any characteristics that would make them distinct cultures. As a result, there is little to distinguish each of the villages from one another.

I suppose that works if you want to show that deep down, all people are the same, but it fails when you try to build a fictional world. Since Sunagakure, there has never been a real sense that each particular hidden village has its own culture, its own way of doing things. Instead, we have focused on individual leaders, robbing the story of its chance to humanize each particular village by fleshing its characters and its culture out as Sunagakure was during the Chunin Exams Arc. Even A, the Raikage's flashback was more about his relationship with Killer B than anything all that revealing about Kumogakure. Darui, A's succesor, whose own flashbacks were simply sentimental rather than informative.

Hell, aside from a bit of meager characterization, there isn't much to say about the good guys from the other hidden villages. The Tsuchikage is full of regret at abandoning his youthful idealism. Also, he's a grouchy old fart. Okay, not too bad. Shame there's no other fleshed out character from his village. Mei, the Mizukage wants to get married. Ao, her bodyguard, is nostalgic for the ninja of his youth. Chojuro, her successor, is shy and likes older women. There's so little to work with, and as a result, Kishimoto's attempts to humanize each of these villages fall flat compared to what they could have been had he taken the time to go into detail about each of them. As a result, the world of Naruto feels awfully small, and not just because whatever maps we've been given are clearly showing only a portion of the entire planet.

Contrast this with its fellow Jump manga One Piece. One criticism that is often voiced in regards to the manga is that the plot is repetitive. What happens is that the Straw Hat Pirates will reach their newest destination, and there, engage in a series of misadventures that are relevant to at the moment current arc, with some of the events taking place having bearing on later plot developments. While there are several shortcomings in Oda's way of writing, I feel that this is not necessarily one of them. What these self-contained subplots do is build up Oda's world. Because they are self-contained, they serve to create the illusion that each individual location has its own story, its own way of life going on. This adds a sense of verisimilitude to what is otherwise a rather surreal world. At the same time, because each of these subplots take place at particular locations, they serve to justify the sense that the main characters' actions will influence the world of One Piece in the near future.

Long story short, while Kishimoto has tried to build his fictional world by offering histories and revealing some characters, his sparse characterization of the villages and their denizens only serves to hinder his attempts to realistically create a living breathing world on the cusp of revolution.

So what is the final verdict? This series is pretty much up to you to love or hate. Personally, I'd recommend this series to people who aren't familiar with manga in general and aren't bothered by the flaws or errors. Otherwise? I'd recommend something better to invest your time on. This manga had potential, but it wasted it. Let us just hope that the next Weekly Shonen Jump phenomenon won't end up as appalling and excruciating as this one. read more

As we approach towards the end of the Naruto, I wanted to express my thoughts on one of the most impactful manga I’ve read to date. First of all, rating this manga is very, very difficult for me because of the sheer number of chapters. The most recent arc (the ninja war) is still fresh in my memory and undoubtedly left a sour taste in my mouth. I’m sure a lot of you agree with me, based on the reactions I’ve seen on multiple platforms. However, I’m going to look at the big picture, and I’m seeing one of the greatest, and certainly, the most polarizing manga ever. Maybe my judgement is clouded, because I started reading this manga since the release of the 7th volume, which was around 10 years ago. I had been reading Naruto on a weekly basis since that day, anxiously waiting for the release of the next chapter. Naruto was very addicting for me because it does a masterful job with cliffhangers and creating suspense at the end of each chapter. I thought that was Kishimoto’s best trait as a writer, which kept me loyal to his manga to this very day. And the best thing about the manga as opposed to the anime is…less milking of the franchise (no fillers).

The concept for Naruto is quite simple and that sits well with the younger readers. Naruto is an underdog story about a misfit boy trying to become the greatest ninja, in a world of ninjas. People like underdogs succeeding. So watching Naruto progress is just an extremely satisfying experience. Watching him succeeding is like those “AHA” moments when you prove someone wrong. This was evident at the end of the Naruto vs. Pain storyline, where the entire village acknowledged him as a hero.

Being approximately 700 chapters long, Kishimoto does an excellent job with a thing I call storyline continuity, far exceeding the likes of Fairy Tail, Bleach, and One Piece. Naruto is one of the few shounens that clearly show character progression through training. The training arcs may be boring at times (I found them to be quite good personally), but when the fight arrives, you know that they had put in the due diligence to become stronger. This makes every fight more rewarding to watch in my opinion. Also, I feel like the arcs in Naruto are more intertwined with each other compared to Bleach and FT, even with the timeskip, because the arcs in those manga feel like they are mutually exclusive to each other. Kishimoto does his best to connect the dots and link each sequence of events from beginning to end. That being said, Naruto is 700 chapters long, and the risk of having plot holes increase the longer the series go. One main criticism of the Naruto manga is the avid use of flashbacks. Going back in time and essentially “whiting out” the details can sometimes be detrimental to the overall story. This is evident in the most recent ninja war arc, where the origins of shinobi and ninjutsu were explained to full effect. The fact that this manga undermines each villain until we reached to the GOD status villain is disappointing, especially with the use of flashbacks. But that does not mean that every flashback was a complete failure. I thought that the Uchiha coup d’état flashback was beautifully executed. Since day one, Sasuke was seeking revenge due to that event, so it was definitely well planned out. Also, Kyuubi’s attack on Konoha was a great example of a good flashback. What do those two events have in common? Both events were extremely emotional, and both were shown or hinted at the very start of the series. The backstory to those tragic moments were perfect pieces to the overall puzzle. Kishimoto had his up and downs with flashbacks, but it’s to be expected from a manga that’s 700 chapters long.

This manga also excels in drawing out your emotions with tear-jerking deaths and heartwarming reunions. The scenes where Naruto interacts with his parents were like 1000 ninjas cutting onions in front of me. You have to be invested in this manga to feel this way, and if you’ve made it that far in the manga, you will feel this way. The deaths in Naruto also do the same thing. Without spoiling anything, I’m glad that Naruto killed off some of their crucial protagonists completely. It’s completely necessary, or the manga won’t be taken as seriously *cough* Fairy Tail. Unfortunately, there is a darker side to all of this. The technique that plagued Naruto to the very end…edo tensei. That “jutsu” definitely erases some of the positives that I just raved to you all. Edo tensei is the biggest deus ex machina asspull of a jutsu that kills the flow of the story and makes deaths less significant. The reason why the entire Shinobi War was a sham of an arc is the perception that reviving almost EVERYONE is good idea. It was a terrible idea, and dragged the war out for far too long.

There are so many characters in the Naruto universe, ranging from great to annoying. Every character in Naruto served a purpose, and fulfilled its role. The down side is, a case can be made for the four main characters of team 7, Naruto, Sasuke, Sakura, and Kakashi to be some of the most annoying characters in all of Naruto. Naruto for his stupidity and his infamous –ttebayo speech impediment. Sasuke and his emo-raging tendencies. Sakura and her illogical obsession with Sasuke combined with her uselessness. Kakashi and his DAMNING mask. Every character is flawed in one way or another, but with so many characters, we get to witness a wide spectrum of personalities and root for the ones we like. I personally love Jiraiya because of his goofiness and what he provides: comedic relief and a father figure for Naruto. The core characters in Naruto all have their internal struggles, and this manga gives them the necessary screen time to show their backstories.

What is the worst thing about this manga? I believe that Kishimoto dropped the ball in terms of ending the story. I am specifically referring to the out of nowhere pairings designed to satisfy the fan base. Creating ridiculous pairings with characters with no prior chemistry with each other is just bufoonish. No spoilers here, but the manga to me, ended on a poor note because of Kishimoto’s desire to satisfy the fan base.
The art for Naruto is certainly unique. I wouldn’t say that Kishimoto is some sort of out-of-this-world artist by any means, but his style of drawing fits the ninja theme quite well. I thought that he emphasized on his drawings in the earlier chapters compared to more recent chapters. Maybe he got lax because he knew that he had already drawn up an empire.

Wow! You made it this far into the review! I didn’t think anyone would care enough to read an insignificant person’s take on Naruto. I’m more or less writing this to preserve my thoughts on a manga that I grew up to. People’s view change over time, but the kid in me says that Naruto is one of the best mangas ever. Those feelings won’t change. read more

It's hard writing this review straight after finishing the manga but I wanted to do it when It's all fresh in my mind. Naruto is a story manga and anime fans over the world know, hate or love, It is easily the most detested manga of all time but yet one of the most popular. The story is about a young boy called Naruto Uzumaki, with his entire village despising and fearing him he is practically alone in the village and because of that he as a character values friendship more than the comman man and that leads to him going above and beyond to bring his best friend Sasuke back. With many arcs and a monumental timeskip Naruto is a long story and not for people who don't have any patience, the land of waves arc, the chunin exams arc, the invasion of konoha arc, the search for tsunade arc and the sasuke retrieval arc comprised was part 1 and the flow of the arcs was great, it had practically no slow points and the emotion is high.

With the land of the waves being the arc that fans usually remember as the arc that hooked them to Naruto with the crazy techiques and really showed of what a ninja is. The chunin exams is a favorite to many and is my favorite arc with crazy battles and learning about many tragic past to characters it is easily the arc that develops more characters than any. The Invasion of konoha arc is something that could of waited till a certain part in the story was concluded but as it was it was an amazing part in the manga and it did bring tears and epic fighting, still could of been handled differently. The search for Tsunade arc is simply the calm before the storm arc, Naruto gets training from Jiraiya and learns the infamous Rasengan while looking for the next Hokage, the dynamics between Naruto, Jiraiya and Tsunade is very well done and the ending of the arc is a pinnacle point in the series. The final arc of part 1 is outstanding to say the least, the battles in this arc are probably the biggest in part 1 and in the series and the final fight is full of emotion which Kishi is well at, overall the arc changed the story but opened up the story for different possibilities.

Each arc focuses on something new and trhat's what I liked about part 1 and how it was starting to build upon the ninja world. Then in part 2 the focus of the story shifted a bit from being a boy who wants to become Hokage to a boy who wants to bring his friend back before he becomes Hokage. Now the idea of that seems great but it did bring allot of complications to the story. In part 2 the arcs were structured not as well but the character dynamic was excellent but did seem more slow at times. The kazekage rescue arc was a good opening as I expected te be fair but then it went downhill with the sai and sasuke arc which is easily the worst arc in the series, the only good thing about it really was Naruto Vs Orochimaru. Moving on the Kakuzu and Hidan arc was an arc which used different characters like ino, shikamaru and Choji, the depth and the meaning of that arc was very well written. The Itachi pursuit arc is possibly the arc with the most emotion, Kishimoto sensei may of changed the story to more character focused than story building focus but that didn't change him still being able to deliver and with 3 huge deaths, 2 massive ones this arc stands out very much so. The nest arc is the Invasion of pain arc which to me is one of the best. I would prefer for certain things to of changed but as Kishimoto sensei did it, I couldn't call it bad writing. The atmosphere in this arc is very dark, it's the most suspenseful in the story so far and the fear of death looms over every character and I like it and the ending was a real changing point in the series and very much developed Naruto's character.

Moving to the more recent arcs the Five Kage summit was something that was needed, we needed for the kages to interact and to show that the shinobi world wasn't perfect and the dynamic between them all proved that the world still needed to change. Events happened in the arc which led to a big change, something that would start a beggining of a new era, overall a great arc. The confining the jinchuriki arc was an interesting one which focused on characters we don't get to see much and going more in depth with them as well as learning more about the past. The 4th shinobi world war arc was slow and the fighting wasn't that good but the ending proved it not to be the worst in the story. The ten tails revival arc was much better as we learn a truth about a certain person and the stakes really are higher, this is one of the best arcs and especially when read in a sitting instead of once a week for 3 years like some. Next is the final arc which could be seen as the best arc in the series as it focused on a select number of characters and it had very shocking moments and overall it concluded the story well and I didn't know if Kishimoto sensei could do it but he did, he concluded it perfectly.
